🌍 Why Do Countries Trade?

Imagine you're great at math but terrible at English. Your friend is great at English but ok at math. You both benefit if you specialize and help each other — that's trade.

Even if one country is better at everything, trade still makes sense. Why? Because of opportunity cost.

🔑 Key Insight
Trade is based on comparative advantage, NOT absolute advantage. Even if Country A is better at producing everything, it should still specialize in what it does with the lowest opportunity cost.

📦 Absolute Advantage

A country has absolute advantage when it can produce MORE output with the same input (or same output with less input) compared to another country.

Compare: Output per hour (or hours per unit)
Higher output per hour = Absolute Advantage
Fewer hours per unit = Absolute Advantage

Example: India takes 40 hrs/motorcycle, Japan takes 60 hrs/motorcycle → India has absolute advantage in motorcycles.

⚖️ Comparative Advantage

A country has comparative advantage when its opportunity cost of producing a good is lower than the other country's.

OC of Good A = Units of B given up to produce 1 unit of A

Even if Japan is worse at both goods, it has comparative advantage in whichever good it gives up LESS to produce.

📌 Exam Rule
Each country has comparative advantage in exactly one good each. They can't both have CA in the same good. This is mathematically guaranteed.

📈 What is PPF?

Production Possibilities Frontier = a line showing all combinations of two goods a country can produce using all its resources efficiently.

Points ON the line = efficient. Inside = inefficient (resources wasted). Outside = impossible (currently).

For simple 2-good models with constant opportunity cost → PPF is a straight line.

💡 Gains from Trade
After trade, a country can CONSUME beyond its PPF — like having more resources. Trade = consumption outside PPF! This is the same effect as technological progress.

🔁 How to Find Opportunity Cost (Step-by-Step)

Given: India: 10 labour hrs/kg Rice, 25 labour hrs/m Cloth | Total: 16000 Lhr

  1. Max Rice India = 16000/10 = 1600 kg | Max Cloth = 16000/25 = 640 m
  2. OC of 1 kg Rice = 640/1600 = 0.4 m Cloth (how much cloth is given up per kg rice)
  3. OC of 1 m Cloth = 1600/640 = 2.5 kg Rice
  4. Do the same for China. Lower OC = Comparative Advantage in that good.
  5. Whoever has lower OC for a good should specialize and export that good.
⚡ Exam Shortcut
OC of Good A = (Max of B) ÷ (Max of A). This is the slope of the PPF. Smaller slope = lower OC = comparative advantage in A.

🏷️ 10 Principles of Economics (from your notes)

Principle 8: A country's standard of living depends on its ability to produce goods & services (= Productivity). Productivity = Q/L = output per worker.

Principle 9: Prices rise when the government prints too much money → Inflation.

Principle 10: Society faces a short-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment (Phillips Curve). ↑ Money supply → ↑ Demand → ↑ Prices → ↑ Production → ↑ Employment.

📌 Phillips Curve
In the short run: High inflation = Low unemployment (and vice versa). This is a trade-off, not a choice you want — society faces it, not chooses it!

🛒 What is CPI?

CPI = Consumer Price Index. It measures how the cost of living changes over time for an average consumer.

Think of it as: "How expensive is the same grocery basket compared to last year?"

💡 Simple Analogy
You buy the same items every month — milk, bread, bus pass. CPI tracks if that fixed bundle is getting more expensive.

📋 5 Steps to Calculate CPI

  1. Fix the Basket — Identify what items consumers buy and in what quantities
  2. Find Prices — Collect prices of each item for each year
  3. Compute Basket Cost — Cost = Σ(Price × Quantity) for each year
  4. Designate Base Year — Usually given; CPI of base year = 100
  5. Compute CPI — CPI = (Cost in current year ÷ Cost in base year) × 100
CPI = (Cost of basket in current year / Cost of basket in base year) × 100
Inflation Rate = [(CPI_yr2 − CPI_yr1) / CPI_yr1] × 100

📉 3 Problems with CPI

CPI overestimates the true cost of living due to 3 biases:

  1. Substitution Bias — When price of beef rises, people buy chicken. CPI ignores this substitution (assumes fixed basket). e.g., Petrol price rises → people buy fuel-efficient cars.
  2. Introduction of New Goods — New products (smartphones) aren't in old basket; their falling prices aren't captured.
  3. Unmeasured Quality Changes — A car today is safer/better than 1990, but CPI may show it as "more expensive" ignoring quality gain.

💵 Nominal vs Real Interest Rate

Nominal Interest Rate = The rate the bank tells you. Does NOT account for inflation.

Real Interest Rate = What you actually earn in purchasing power.

Real Interest Rate = Nominal Rate − Inflation Rate
Nominal Rate = [(Final Amt − Initial Amt) / Initial Amt] × 100
📌 Memory Trick
NIR = what bank promises. RIR = what you actually get in purchasing power. Deflation makes RIR > NIR!

📏 Adjusting Values Across Time

To convert a past salary/fee/price into today's money:

Today's Value = Past Value × (CPI_today ÷ CPI_past)

Example from notes: Sportsman earned $80,000 in 1931. CPI 1931 = 15.2, CPI 2012 = 229.5.
2012 equivalent = 80,000 × (229.5/15.2) = $1,207,894

Assignment Example: Uncle earned ₹25,000 in 1980. CPI 1980 = 82, CPI 2025 = 246.
2025 equivalent = 25,000 × (246/82) = 25,000 × 3 = ₹75,000

📌 In Exam
"What is the 2025 equivalent of ₹X earned in year Y?" → Use formula above. Also works for tuition fees, salaries, etc.

🏦 What is GDP?

Gross Domestic Product = Total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given time period.

Key words: final (not intermediate), within borders (not by citizens abroad), in a year.

Inclusion Criteria: Imputed rent (owner-occupied homes), inventory additions, govt employee salaries, construction costs on vehicles/machinery.

Exclusion Criteria: Second-hand goods, transfer payments (pensions, unemployment allowance), goods produced & consumed within household (backyard vegetables), underground economy.

📊 3 Approaches to GDP

Y = C + I + G + NX (Expenditure Approach)
  • C = Consumption (households buying goods/services, EXCEPT new house)
  • I = Investment (firms buying capital + new housing + inventory changes)
  • G = Government Purchases (NOT transfer payments like pensions)
  • NX = Net Exports = Exports − Imports
GDP = Wage + Rent + Interest + Profit (Income Approach)
GDP = Σ Value Added at each stage (Value-Added Approach)

📈 Nominal vs Real GDP

Nominal GDP = Output valued at current year prices. Rises even if production doesn't (due to inflation).

Real GDP = Output valued at base year prices. Shows TRUE change in production.

Nominal GDP = Σ (Current Price × Current Quantity)
Real GDP = Σ (Base Year Price × Current Quantity)
⚠️ Base Year Rule
In the base year: Nominal GDP = Real GDP. GDP Deflator = 100. Always!

🔢 GDP Deflator

GDP Deflator measures the overall price level relative to base year. Unlike CPI, it covers ALL domestically produced goods.

GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP ÷ Real GDP) × 100
Inflation Rate = [(Deflator_yr2 − Deflator_yr1) / Deflator_yr1] × 100
📌 CPI vs GDP Deflator Key Diff
CPI: fixed basket, includes imports, tracks consumer goods only.
GDP Deflator: all domestic production, changes with output mix, excludes imports.

🔗 Value-Added Approach (Worked Example)

From your notes: A farmer grows wheat → sells to miller for ₹80,000 → miller turns to flour → sells to baker for ₹1,00,000 → baker makes bread → sells to consumer for ₹1,50,000.

StageSale PriceInput CostValue Added
Farmer (wheat)₹80,000₹0₹80,000
Miller (flour)₹1,00,000₹80,000₹20,000

GDP = Final Price = Sum of Value Added₹1,50,000

Notice: All three approaches give the same answer: ₹1,50,000. This is not a coincidence — it's accounting identity.

💰 Saving & Investment Identity

In a closed economy (no trade), all savings must equal investment.

Y = C + I + G (closed economy)
National Saving (S) = Y − C − G
Private Saving = Y − T − C (disposable income − consumption)
Public Saving = T − G (tax revenue − govt spending)
National Saving = Private + Public = I

Budget Surplus: T > G → Positive public saving
Budget Deficit: T < G → Negative public saving (govt borrows, "crowding out")

📊 GDP Per Capita & Standard of Living

GDP per capita = GDP ÷ Population. It measures average income per person — a better indicator of standard of living than total GDP.

💡 Example from Assignment
India's total GDP >> Singapore's. But Singapore's GDP per capita >> India's. Higher living standard in Singapore despite lower total GDP!

Also: Higher GDP per capita does NOT always = higher economic growth rate.
GDP per capita = Real GDP / Total Population
Economic Growth Rate = [(GDP_yr2 - GDP_yr1) / GDP_yr1] × 100

🌱 4 Sources of Productivity (Determinants)

  • Physical Capital (K) — Tools, machines, buildings. ↑K/L → ↑productivity. But diminishing returns!
  • Human Capital (H) — Education, skills, training. Investment sacrifices current consumption for future wages.
  • Natural Resources (N) — Land, minerals, oil. Renewable (solar, water) vs Non-renewable (coal, oil).
  • Technological Knowledge (A) — Society's understanding of best production methods. Key driver of long-run growth.
y = Af(L, H, K, N) — Production Function

🤝 Trade = Technology

Trade allows countries to consume beyond their PPF, just like technology expands the PPF. Both increase productivity without new resources.

If India specializes in rice and China in cloth, both can consume MORE — same as if technology improved.

⚠️ Capital Accumulation is NOT Free Lunch
More capital → More output, BUT diminishing returns. To produce more capital, you must sacrifice consumption now (save more). And each new unit of capital adds less than the previous → cannot sustain growth forever. Need technological progress!

🏦 Loanable Funds Market

Supply = National Saving (households save → supply loanable funds)

Demand = Businesses/households borrow to invest

Price = Real Interest Rate (equilibrium where S = I)

Saving Incentives: Tax on interest → discourages saving → supply shifts LEFT → interest rate ↑, loanable funds ↓

Investment Incentives: Tax credit for new capital → demand shifts RIGHT → interest rate ↑, loanable funds ↑

Budget Deficit: Govt borrows → supply shifts LEFT (national saving ↓) → interest rate ↑ → crowds out private investment

🏛️ What is the Financial System?

A group of institutions that help match one person's saving with another person's investment.

Financial Markets (direct — savers directly provide funds):

  • Bond Market (debt certificates)
  • Stock Market (equity ownership)

Financial Intermediaries (indirect — savers provide to institution → institution lends):

  • Commercial Banks, Mutual Funds, NBFCs

📄 Bonds: Key Characteristics

  1. Term — Length until maturity. Long-term bonds are riskier → higher interest rate
  2. Credit Risk — Probability of default. Higher risk → higher interest demanded. Junk bonds = high risk, high interest
  3. Tax Treatment — Municipal bonds (state/local govt) are tax-exempt → lower interest rate
💡 Perpetuity
A bond with NO maturity date — pays interest forever. British "Consol" bonds issued in 1917 are perpetuities. Principal never repaid!

📊 Mutual Funds

Sells shares to public → uses proceeds to buy portfolio of stocks/bonds.

Principle: "Don't put all eggs in one basket" = diversification reduces risk.

Index Funds (subset of mutual funds): Buy all stocks in a given index. Outperform most actively managed funds because they keep costs low by rarely trading.

🔄 Budget Deficit & Crowding Out

When govt runs a deficit, it borrows in the loanable funds market → ↑ demand for funds → ↑ interest rate → businesses borrow LESS → private investment ↓.

This is called crowding out.

⚠️ Mechanism
Deficit → ↓ Public Saving → ↓ National Saving → Supply of loanable funds shifts LEFT → ↑ interest rate → ↓ private investment → Long-run: less capital → slower growth

💴 What is Money?

Money = set of assets people regularly use to buy goods & services. It's NOT just cash!

Money Supply (M1) = Currency (C) + Demand Deposits (D)

Currency = Paper bills and coins held by the public

Demand Deposits = Bank account balances accessible by cheque/debit card

Money Supply = Currency + Demand Deposits

🏦 Fractional Reserve Banking

Banks keep a fraction of deposits as reserves and lend out the rest.

Reserve Ratio (R) = Fraction of deposits held as reserve (set by central bank).

Money Multiplier (m) = 1/R = 1/Reserve Ratio
Total Money Supply = Initial Deposit × Money Multiplier

Example: R = 10% (0.10). Initial deposit = $100. Multiplier = 1/0.10 = 10. Money supply = $100 × 10 = $1,000.

🏛️ Bank T-Account

A simplified accounting statement showing a bank's assets & liabilities.

AssetsLiabilities
Reserves = $10 (10%)Deposits = $100
Loans = $90

Money Supply = C + D = $90 (loans back in system) + $100 = $190

📌 100% Reserve System
Banks keep ALL deposits as reserves → no loans → money supply = just the initial deposit (no money creation).

Graph 1 — Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF)

When to draw: Any question with "construct PPF", "production combination", absolute/comparative advantage.

📐 How to Draw (Step-by-Step)

  1. Find Max of Good A: Total resources ÷ resources needed per unit of A
  2. Find Max of Good B: Total resources ÷ resources needed per unit of B
  3. Plot endpoints: (Max A, 0) on x-axis and (0, Max B) on y-axis
  4. Connect with straight line (constant OC = straight line PPF)
  5. Mark production point if they produce both goods (use % allocation)
Slope of PPF = −(Max of Y-axis good) ÷ (Max of X-axis good)
Slope magnitude = OC of x-axis good in terms of y-axis good
⚠️ Common Mistake
Don't plot "production hours" on the axes — plot the quantity of goods! Axes = Goods produced, not resources.
📌 70% Allocation Example
Austria: Max 40 cakes or 80 candles. 2000 hrs total.
70% to cakes → 1400 hrs → (2000×40/2000)×0.7 = 28 cakes
30% to candles → 600 hrs → (2000×80/2000)×0.3 = 24 candles
Plot point (28, 24) on the PPF.

Straight line PPF = Constant opportunity cost. The slope of the line = OC of the x-axis good.

Graph 2 — Two PPFs: Comparative Advantage Diagram

When comparing two countries, draw both PPFs on the same axes. The one with the less steep slope for a good has comparative advantage in that good.

How to Read:

  • If India's PPF is flatter for x-axis good → India has CA in x-good
  • If Japan's PPF is steeper → Japan has CA in y-good
  • The outer PPF (higher intercepts) = Absolute advantage in that good
📌 Exam Read
Steeper slope of PPF = Higher OC = Comparative DISadvantage in that good. Always check BOTH intercepts to determine absolute advantage.
💡 Before & After Trade
Before trade: production point = consumption point (on PPF)
After trade with specialization: production point stays on PPF, but CONSUMPTION point can be BEYOND PPF!

Graph 3 — Loanable Funds Market: Policy Shifts

Saving Incentive (e.g., tax on interest reduced)

Supply shifts RIGHT → interest rate ↓ → investment ↑

Investment Incentive (e.g., tax credit)

Demand shifts RIGHT → interest rate ↑ → saving ↑

Budget Deficit (crowding out)

Supply shifts LEFT → interest rate ↑ → private investment crowded out

Graph 4 — CPI vs GDP Deflator: Key Differences

🛒 CPI
  • Fixed basket (base year quantities)
  • Includes imported goods (e.g., Yamaha bike made in Japan, sold in India)
  • Excludes some domestically produced goods (not in basket)
  • Overestimates inflation (substitution bias)
  • Used for adjusting wages, pensions
VS
🏭 GDP Deflator
  • Current year basket (changes every year)
  • Excludes imports (only domestic production)
  • Includes ALL domestic goods, even those not in consumer basket
  • More accurate reflection of domestic price level
  • Used for measuring economic growth
📌 Exam Trick — Assignment Q6 Type
  • Bajaj Sugar ↑ price (produced & sold in India) → Affects BOTH CPI and GDP Deflator
  • L&T increases price of Komatsu excavator (made & sold in India) → Affects GDP Deflator (capital good), NOT CPI (not in consumer basket)
  • Yamaha MT-10 (made in Japan, sold in India) → Affects CPI (imported consumer good), NOT GDP Deflator (not domestically produced)

Graph 5 — Phillips Curve (Principle 10)

The Phillips Curve shows the short-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment.

How it works:

  1. Govt prints more money → ↑ money supply
  2. ↑ demand for goods → ↑ prices (inflation)
  3. ↑ production → firms hire more labour
  4. ↓ unemployment (at least in short run)
⚠️ Important Distinction
This is a SHORT-RUN trade-off only. In the long run, the Phillips Curve is vertical — you can't permanently reduce unemployment by accepting more inflation.

📦 Trade & Opportunity Cost Formulas

▼
OC of Good A = Max output of B / Max output of A

When to use: Whenever asked for comparative advantage, basis of trade, who should specialize in what.

When NOT to use: Don't use this for absolute advantage — for AA, just compare max outputs or hours per unit directly.

Slope of PPF = −(Y-intercept) / (X-intercept)

When to use: To find OC graphically. The magnitude of slope = OC of x-axis good in terms of y-axis good.

Solved Example: India can produce max 1600 kg Rice or 640 m Cloth. OC of 1 kg Rice = 640/1600 = 0.4 m Cloth. OC of 1 m Cloth = 1600/640 = 2.5 kg Rice.

🛒 CPI & Inflation Formulas

▼
CPI = (Cost of basket in current year / Cost of basket in base year) × 100
Inflation Rate (%) = [(CPI_yr2 − CPI_yr1) / CPI_yr1] × 100
Equivalent Value = Old Value × (CPI_now / CPI_then)

Worked Example (Assignment Q8): Uncle earned ₹25,000 in 1980. CPI 1980=82, CPI 2025=246.
Equivalent salary = 25,000 × (246/82) = 25,000 × 3 = ₹75,000

When NOT to use CPI formula: Don't use CPI formula to compute deflation for GDP or to measure economy-wide price changes — use GDP Deflator instead.

💵 Nominal vs Real Interest Rate

▼
Nominal Interest Rate (NIR) = [(Final Amt − Initial Amt) / Initial Amt] × 100
Real Interest Rate (RIR) = NIR − Inflation Rate

Worked Example (Assignment Q5): Deposit ₹2,000 in 2024 → grows to ₹2,200 in 2025. CPI rises from 200 to 220.

  • NIR = [(2200−2000)/2000] × 100 = 10%
  • Inflation = [(220−200)/200] × 100 = 10%
  • RIR = 10% − 10% = 0% (no real gain!)

Worked Example (Assignment Q6): Mahesh lends ₹100, receives ₹130. CPI increased 20%.

  • NIR = [(130−100)/100] × 100 = 30%
  • Inflation = 20%
  • RIR = 30% − 20% = 10%

🏦 GDP Formulas

▼
Nominal GDP = Σ (Current Price × Current Quantity)
Real GDP = Σ (Base Year Price × Current Quantity)
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100
Inflation Rate via Deflator = [(Def_yr2 − Def_yr1) / Def_yr1] × 100
Y = C + I + G + NX (Expenditure approach)
GDP per capita = Real GDP / Population

When to use Real GDP: Comparing economic growth over time, measuring standard of living.

When to use Nominal GDP: Computing GDP Deflator, current market value calculations.

📌 Key Rule
In base year: Nominal GDP = Real GDP → GDP Deflator = 100. This is always true, no exceptions!

💰 Saving & Investment Formulas

▼
Private Saving = Y − T − C (income minus taxes minus consumption)
Public Saving = T − G (taxes minus govt spending)
National Saving (S) = Y − C − G = Private + Public Saving
In closed economy: S = I (National Saving = Investment)
Disposable Income = Y − T

Worked Example (Assignment 2, Q2):

GDP (Y) = $200,000; C = $100,000; G = $40,000; Budget Surplus = $10,000 (means T − G = 10,000 → T = $50,000)

  • Disposable Income = Y − T = 200,000 − 50,000 = $150,000
  • Private Saving = Y − T − C = 200,000 − 50,000 − 100,000 = $50,000
  • Public Saving = T − G = 50,000 − 40,000 = $10,000
  • National Saving = 50,000 + 10,000 = $60,000
  • Investment = National Saving = $60,000

🏦 Money Multiplier

▼
Money Multiplier (m) = 1 / Reserve Ratio (R)
Total Money Supply = Initial Deposit × (1/R)

Worked Example: R = 10%, initial deposit = $100. m = 1/0.10 = 10. Money supply = $100 × 10 = $1,000.

If R increases to 20%: m = 1/0.20 = 5. Money supply = $100 × 5 = $500. ↑R → ↓Money supply.

MIDSEM Q1a Rohan & Mohan produce TV and Laptop. TV: Rohan=40 hrs, Mohan=80 hrs. Laptop: Rohan=80 hrs, Mohan=40 hrs. Who has comparative advantage in TV and Laptop? ▼

Concept: Comparative Advantage = Lower Opportunity Cost

PersonTV (hrs)Laptop (hrs)OC of TVOC of Laptop
Rohan408080/40 = 2 Laptops40/80 = 0.5 TVs
Mohan804040/80 = 0.5 Laptops80/40 = 2 TVs

Conclusion:

  • TV: Mohan's OC (0.5 Laptops) < Rohan's OC (2 Laptops) → Mohan has CA in TV
  • Laptop: Rohan's OC (0.5 TVs) < Mohan's OC (2 TVs) → Rohan has CA in Laptop
💡 Note
Mohan is LESS efficient at both goods (takes more hours for both) — yet trade still benefits both! Mohan should produce TVs, Rohan should produce Laptops.
MIDSEM Q1b India: 1800 T-shirts or 600 pairs of trousers. USA: 2000 T-shirts or 600 pairs. Compute total opportunity cost and unit opportunity cost of producing trousers by an Indian. ▼

Total Opportunity Cost of producing 600 trousers by India:

To produce 600 trousers, India gives up ALL T-shirt production = 1800 T-shirts

Unit Opportunity Cost of 1 pair of trousers:

OC of 1 trouser = 1800/600 = 3 T-shirts

Unit OC of Trousers = Max T-shirts / Max Trousers = 1800/600 = 3 T-shirts

For USA: OC of 1 trouser = 2000/600 = 3.33 T-shirts

Comparative Advantage: India's OC (3) < USA's OC (3.33) → India has CA in trousers. USA has CA in T-shirts.

MIDSEM Q1c Bijaya has 40 labour hours/week. OC of 1 kg potatoes = 0.25 kg meat. PPF touches potatoes axis at 40 units. Draw the PPF. ▼

Given: X-axis = Potatoes (max = 40 units), OC of 1 kg potatoes = 0.25 kg meat

Find Y-intercept (max meat):

OC of 1 kg potatoes = 0.25 kg meat means: to produce 40 kg potatoes, Bijaya gives up 40 × 0.25 = 10 kg meat

So max meat = 10 kg. Y-intercept = (0, 10). X-intercept = (40, 0).

PPF: Straight line from (0, 10) on meat axis to (40, 0) on potato axis.

📌 Slope Verification
Slope = −10/40 = −0.25. Magnitude 0.25 = OC of potatoes in terms of meat. ✓ Consistent!
MIDSEM Q2 (Hotland CPI) Hotland residents buy cauliflower, broccoli, carrots. 2020: 100 cauliflower @$2, 50 broccoli @$1.50, 500 carrots @$0.10. 2021: prices changed. Compute CPI and inflation rate. ▼

Step 1 — Find prices per unit in each year:

Broccoli$75/50 = $1.50$120/80 = $1.5050
Carrots$50/500 = $0.10$100/500 = $0.20500

  • 2020: (100×2) + (50×1.5) + (500×0.10) = 200 + 75 + 50 = $325

MIDSEM Q3a (GDP Pizza/Burger) Pizza: 2020 $20/400, 2021 $21/500, 2022 $22/600. Burger: 2020 $12/1000, 2021 $13/1100, 2022 $14/1200. Compute Nominal GDP, Real GDP, GDP Deflator (base 2020). ▼

Nominal GDP (current prices × current qty):

  • 2020: (20×400)+(12×1000) = 8000+12000 = $20,000
  • 2021: (21×500)+(13×1100) = 10500+14300 = $24,800
  • 2022: (22×600)+(14×1200) = 13200+16800 = $30,000

Real GDP (base year 2020 prices × current qty):

  • 2020: (20×400)+(12×1000) = $20,000
  • 2021: (20×500)+(12×1100) = 10000+13200 = $23,200
  • 2022: (20×600)+(12×1200) = 12000+14400 = $26,400

GDP Deflator = (Nominal/Real) × 100:

  • 2020: (20,000/20,000)×100 = 100
  • 2021: (24,800/23,200)×100 = 106.9
  • 2022: (30,000/26,400)×100 = 113.6

Inflation Rate: 2020-21: [(106.9-100)/100]×100 = 6.9% | 2021-22: [(113.6-106.9)/106.9]×100 = 6.27%

MIDSEM Q5b GDP = $7 trillion, Tax Revenue = $2 trillion, Private Saving = $0.4 trillion, Public Saving = $0.3 trillion. Find Consumption, Govt Purchases, National Saving, Investment. ▼

Given: Y = 7T, T = 2T, Private Saving = 0.4T, Public Saving = 0.3T (closed economy)

H-02 "An increase in petrol price leads to greater use of fuel-efficient cars." Which problem in CPI construction does this illustrate? [Assign 1 · Q10] Hard ▼

Problem Illustrated: Substitution Bias

When petrol prices rise, rational consumers substitute by buying fuel-efficient cars (use less petrol). They adapt their consumption bundle to minimize cost.

However, CPI uses a fixed basket from the base year. If the base year basket assumed consumers buy regular cars and lots of petrol, it does NOT account for the shift to fuel-efficient cars.

The CPI calculation will show petrol as very expensive, BUT consumers are actually buying LESS petrol (by switching cars). So CPI overestimates the true cost of living because it doesn't reflect this rational substitution.

💡 In Exam
Whenever you see "price of X rises → consumers shift to Y," that's substitution bias in CPI. CPI ignores this behavioral change because it uses a fixed basket.
H-03 "GDP is a good measure of economic well-being, but not a perfect one." Analyse. [Assign 2 · Q7, Midsem Q4a] Hard ▼

Why GDP IS a good measure (arguments for):

  • Countries with high GDP per capita tend to have better healthcare, education, nutrition
  • Higher GDP → more government revenue → better public services
  • Widely used, standardized, easy to compare across countries
  • Strongly correlated with HDI (Human Development Index)

H-04 "CPI is an imperfect measure of the cost of living." Analyse. [Assign 2 · Q8] Hard ▼

Three biases that make CPI imperfect:

  1. Substitution Bias: CPI uses a fixed basket. When one good becomes expensive, consumers substitute. CPI doesn't capture this → overestimates cost of living.
    Example: Petrol price rises → people buy fuel-efficient cars → actual spending on petrol falls, but CPI still assumes old consumption pattern.
  2. New Goods Bias: When new goods (smartphones, streaming services) are introduced, consumers can buy them cheaply. CPI basket doesn't immediately include them → misses the value of new goods.
  3. Quality Bias: A laptop in 2025 is far better than 2010, but if price rose, CPI records it as more expensive without accounting for quality improvement → overestimates price increase.

Overall Effect: All three biases cause CPI to overstate inflation and therefore overstate the cost of living. Real wages (adjusted for CPI) may be understated.

H-05 "Trade has a similar effect as that of technology." Analyse. [Assign 2 · Q9] Hard ▼

How Technology affects the economy:

  • New technology allows a country to produce MORE with the same resources
  • Graphically: PPF shifts outward → can consume more of both goods
  • Improves productivity without adding more labour or capital

How Trade creates similar effect:

  • When countries specialize based on comparative advantage and trade, they can CONSUME combinations of goods that lie BEYOND their own PPF

H-06 "Economic growth that arises from capital accumulation is not a free lunch." Analyse. [Assign 2 · Q10, Midsem Q4a] Hard ▼

Why it's not free:

Reason 1 — Sacrifice of current consumption: To produce more capital goods (machines, factories), a society must divert resources away from consumer goods. This means less consumption NOW to get more production LATER.

Reason 2 — Diminishing Returns to Capital:

  • 1st unit of capital → large increase in output
  • 2nd unit → smaller increase
  • 3rd unit → even smaller increase
  • As K/L increases, each additional unit of capital adds LESS productivity

Reason 3 — Catch-up effect but not sustained: Capital accumulation alone cannot sustain long-run growth. Eventually, additional capital barely adds to output. Sustained growth requires technological progress.

Conclusion: Capital accumulation is not "free" — it requires giving up current consumption (saving more) and faces diminishing returns. The cost is the sacrifice of current well-being. But it IS necessary for growth, just not sufficient by itself.

🟡 LIKELY — Nominal vs Real Interest Rate (4 marks)

LIKELY

Expected Question: "Deposit X, receive Y after 1 year. CPI changes from A to B. Find nominal rate, inflation rate, real interest rate."

Model Answer (3 steps):

  1. NIR = [(Y−X)/X] × 100
  2. Inflation = [(CPI_B − CPI_A)/CPI_A] × 100
  3. RIR = NIR − Inflation Rate

Interpretation: If RIR = 0 → no real gain. If RIR > 0 → real purchasing power grew. If RIR < 0 → inflation eroded the deposit's value!

⚠️ Section 8

Confusion Breaker

The most common mistakes — clarified once and for all.

Absolute Advantage vs Comparative Advantage — The MOST Confused Pair

▼
Absolute Advantage
  • Compare outputs directly (more output = AA)
  • OR compare hours directly (fewer hours = AA)
  • A country CAN have AA in BOTH goods
  • Based on productivity comparison
  • NOT the basis of trade (by itself)
VS
Comparative Advantage
  • Compare opportunity costs (lower OC = CA)
  • A country CANNOT have CA in both goods
  • Based on what you give up, not how good you are
  • THE basis of trade (always)
  • Even weaker country has CA in ONE good

CPI vs GDP Deflator — Endless Confusion

▼
CPI
  • Fixed basket (base year quantities)
  • Includes IMPORTS (what consumers buy)
  • Does NOT include all domestically produced goods
  • Consumer goods only
  • Overestimates inflation (substitution bias)
VS
GDP Deflator
  • Changing basket (current year quantities)
  • Excludes IMPORTS (only domestic production)
  • Includes ALL domestically produced goods
  • Capital goods, consumer goods, govt goods
  • More accurate for overall price level
📌 Super Simple Rule
CPI = "What Indians BUY" (includes imports)
GDP Deflator = "What India PRODUCES" (excludes imports)

Nominal vs Real GDP — Never Confuse These Again

▼
Nominal GDP
  • Current year prices × Current quantities
  • Changes due to BOTH price changes AND output changes
  • Higher in current year (inflation makes it look bigger)
  • Use for: Computing deflator, current market value
VS
Real GDP
  • Base year prices × Current quantities
  • Changes ONLY due to output changes
  • Accurate measure of economic growth
  • Use for: Comparing growth over time

Key insight: In base year, NGDP = RGDP = Deflator of 100. In later years, if NGDP > RGDP → there was inflation (prices are higher than base year).

▼

Private SavingY − T − CHouseholds & firmsAlways positive (if T < disposable income)
Public SavingT − GGovernmentT > G (budget surplus)

⚠️ Don't Confuse
Public Saving is NOT govt revenue alone — it's T − G. If govt runs a deficit (T < G), public saving is NEGATIVE. This reduces national saving → less investment → lower long-run growth (crowding out).

Investment in Economics ≠ Investment in Everyday Life

▼

Common Mistake: Students think buying stocks or mutual funds = Investment in GDP.

Investment IN GDP (I)
  • Business spending on capital equipment
  • Construction of new residential housing
  • Changes in business inventories
  • Repair costs of machinery/vehicles
≠
NOT Investment in GDP
  • Buying stocks or bonds (financial investment)
  • Buying second-hand equipment
  • Purchase of old house
  • Government transfer payments

Slope of PPF = Opportunity Cost (Don't Get This Wrong!)

▼

PPF: X-axis = Good A, Y-axis = Good B

Slope = −(Y-intercept) / (X-intercept) = −(Max B) / (Max A)

MAGNITUDE of slope = OC of Good A (x-axis good) in terms of Good B

📌 Key Rule
OC of X-axis good = |Slope| = Max Y / Max X
OC of Y-axis good = Max X / Max Y (the reciprocal)
Country with LESS STEEP PPF has lower OC of x-axis good = comparative advantage in x-axis good

Ultra-compact, exam-ready summaries. Read 30 minutes before your exam.

🌍 Trade & PPF
  • AA = fewer inputs / more output than others
  • CA = lower opportunity cost than others
  • Trade based on CA, not AA
  • CA in both goods = IMPOSSIBLE
  • PPF slope = OC of x-axis good

  • 5 steps: Fix basket → Prices → Cost → Base → CPI
  • CPI = (Current Cost / Base Cost) × 100
  • Base year CPI = 100 always
  • 3 biases: Substitution, New goods, Quality
  • Inflation = [(CPI2 - CPI1)/CPI1] × 100
  • Salary adj = Old × (CPI_new / CPI_old)
  • CPI includes imports; Deflator doesn't

  • Y = C + I + G + NX
  • Final goods only (no intermediate)
  • Nominal = Current prices × Current qty
  • Real = Base prices × Current qty
  • Deflator = (Nominal / Real) × 100
  • Base year: Nominal = Real; Deflator = 100
  • Value Added = Final Price − Input Cost
💵 Interest Rates
  • NIR = [(Final−Initial)/Initial] × 100
  • Inflation = [(CPI2−CPI1)/CPI1] × 100
  • RIR = NIR − Inflation Rate
  • RIR = 0 → no real gain
  • RIR < 0 → inflation ate your savings
💰 Saving & Investment
  • Private Saving = Y − T − C
  • Public Saving = T − G
  • National Saving = Y − C − G
  • S = I in closed economy
  • Surplus: T > G → positive public saving
  • Deficit: T < G → crowding out
  • Investment incentive → Demand shifts right
  • Saving incentive → Supply shifts right
📊 GDP Components
  • C: households, NOT new house
  • I: firms + new housing + inventory
  • G: govt purchases, NOT transfers
  • NX = Exports − Imports
  • Foreign spending in India = +NX = +GDP
