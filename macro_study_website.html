🌍 Why Do Countries Trade?

Imagine you're great at math but terrible at English. Your friend is great at English but ok at math. You both benefit if you specialize and help each other — that's trade.

Even if one country is better at everything, trade still makes sense. Why? Because of opportunity cost.

🔑 Key Insight
Trade is based on comparative advantage, NOT absolute advantage. Even if Country A is better at producing everything, it should still specialize in what it does with the lowest opportunity cost.

📦 Absolute Advantage

A country has absolute advantage when it can produce MORE output with the same input (or same output with less input) compared to another country.

Compare: Output per hour (or hours per unit)
Higher output per hour = Absolute Advantage
Fewer hours per unit = Absolute Advantage

Example: India takes 40 hrs/motorcycle, Japan takes 60 hrs/motorcycle → India has absolute advantage in motorcycles.

⚖️ Comparative Advantage

A country has comparative advantage when its opportunity cost of producing a good is lower than the other country's.

OC of Good A = Units of B given up to produce 1 unit of A

Even if Japan is worse at both goods, it has comparative advantage in whichever good it gives up LESS to produce.

📌 Exam Rule
Each country has comparative advantage in exactly one good each. They can't both have CA in the same good.

📈 What is PPF?

Production Possibilities Frontier = a line showing all combinations of two goods a country can produce using all its resources efficiently.

Points ON the line = efficient. Inside = inefficient (resources wasted). Outside = impossible (currently).

For simple 2-good models with constant opportunity cost → PPF is a straight line.

💡 Real Life
A country can't produce unlimited of everything. If India produces more cars, it must produce fewer phones — that's the PPF tradeoff.

🔁 How to Find Opportunity Cost (Step-by-Step)

Given: India: 10 labour hrs to produce 1 kg Rice, 25 labour hrs for 1 m Cloth | Total: 16000 Lhr

  1. Max Rice India = 16000/10 = 1600 kg | Max Cloth = 16000/25 = 640 m
  2. OC of 1 kg Rice = 640/1600 = 0.4 m Cloth (how much cloth is given up per kg rice)
  3. OC of 1 m Cloth = 1600/640 = 2.5 kg Rice
  4. Do the same for China. Lower OC = Comparative Advantage in that good.
  5. Conclusion: Whoever has lower OC for a good should specialize and export that good.
⚡ Exam Shortcut
OC of Good A = (Max of B) ÷ (Max of A). This is the slope of the PPF. Smaller slope = lower OC = comparative advantage in A.

🛒 What is CPI?

CPI = Consumer Price Index. It measures how the cost of living changes over time for an average consumer.

Think of it as: "How expensive is the same grocery basket compared to last year?"

💡 Simple Analogy
You buy the same items every month — milk, bread, bus pass. CPI tracks if that fixed bundle is getting more expensive.

📋 5 Steps to Calculate CPI

  1. Fix the Basket — Identify what items consumers buy and in what quantities
  2. Find Prices — Collect prices of each item for each year
  3. Compute Basket Cost — Cost = Σ(Price × Quantity) for each year
  4. Designate Base Year — Usually given; CPI of base year = 100
  5. Compute CPI — CPI = (Cost in current year ÷ Cost in base year) × 100

📉 Problems with CPI

CPI overestimates the true cost of living due to 3 biases:

  1. Substitution Bias — When price of beef rises, people buy chicken. CPI ignores this substitution (assumes fixed basket).
  2. Introduction of New Goods — New products (smartphones) aren't in old basket; their falling prices aren't captured.
  3. Unmeasured Quality Changes — A car today is safer/better than 1990, but CPI may show it as "more expensive" ignoring quality gain.

💵 Nominal vs Real Interest Rate

Nominal Interest Rate = The rate the bank tells you. Does NOT account for inflation.

Real Interest Rate = What you actually earn in purchasing power.

Real Interest Rate = Nominal Rate − Inflation Rate
Nominal Rate = (Final Amt − Initial Amt) / Initial Amt × 100

📏 Equivalent Current Salary / Adjusting for Inflation

When someone earned a salary in a past year and you want to find its value in today's money:

Equivalent Current Salary = Old Salary × (CPIcurrent ÷ CPIold)

Example: Uncle earned ₹25,000 in 1980. CPI 1980 = 82, CPI 2025 = 246.
Equivalent 2025 salary = 25000 × (246/82) = 25000 × 3 = ₹75,000

📌 In Exam — If They Ask
"What is the 2025 equivalent of ₹X earned in year Y?" → Use the formula above. This is CO2 topic.

🏦 What is GDP?

Gross Domestic Product = Total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given time period.

Key words: final (not intermediate), within borders (not by citizens abroad), in a year.

💡 Think of it as
GDP = The total score of a country's economic output in one year. Higher = richer economy.

📊 GDP Components (Expenditure Approach)

Y = C + I + G + NX
  • C = Consumption (households buying goods/services)
  • I = Investment (firms buying capital + housing + inventory)
  • G = Government Purchases (NOT transfers like pensions)
  • NX = Net Exports = Exports − Imports

📈 Nominal vs Real GDP

Nominal GDP = Output valued at current year prices. Rises even if production doesn't (due to inflation).

Real GDP = Output valued at base year prices. Shows TRUE change in production.

Nominal GDP = Σ (Current Price × Current Quantity)
Real GDP = Σ (Base Year Price × Current Quantity)

🔢 GDP Deflator

GDP Deflator measures the overall price level relative to base year. Unlike CPI, it covers ALL domestically produced goods.

GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP ÷ Real GDP) × 100

GDP Deflator of base year = 100 always. Above 100 = inflation; below 100 = deflation.

📌 CPI vs GDP Deflator — Key Diff
CPI: fixed basket, includes imports, tracks consumer goods only
GDP Deflator: all domestic production, changes with output mix

🔗 Value Added Approach

To avoid double counting, GDP only counts value added at each stage.

Example: Farmer sells wheat to Miller for ₹80,000 → Miller sells flour to Baker for ₹1,00,000 → Baker sells bread for ₹1,50,000 to consumers.

StageSale PriceInput CostValue Added
Farmer (wheat)₹80,000₹0₹80,000
Miller (flour)₹1,00,000₹80,000₹20,000
Baker (bread)₹1,50,000₹1,00,000₹50,000
GDP₹1,50,000

Notice: GDP = Final price = Sum of value added = ₹1,50,000. Three methods, same answer!

💰 Saving & Investment Identity

In a closed economy (no trade), all savings must equal investment. Why? Because every dollar saved = dollar available for borrowing = dollar invested.

Y = C + I + G (closed economy)
National Saving (S) = Y − C − G = I
Private Saving = Y − T − C
Public Saving = T − G

📊 GDP Per Capita

GDP per capita = GDP ÷ Population. It measures average income per person — a better indicator of standard of living than total GDP.

💡 Example
India's GDP > Singapore's GDP (India is bigger). But Singapore's GDP per capita >> India's (more income per person). Higher living standard in Singapore!
📌 In Exam
"Higher GDP = Higher living standard?" → NOT necessarily. Check GDP per capita and also non-monetary factors (healthcare, leisure, etc.)

🌱 Sources of Economic Growth

  • Physical Capital — Tools, machines, buildings
  • Human Capital — Education, skills, training
  • Natural Resources — Land, minerals, oil
  • Technological Knowledge — Know-how, R&D
⚠️ Key Concept
"Capital accumulation is not a free lunch" — Diminishing returns mean each additional unit of capital adds less and less growth. Sustained growth requires technology.

🤝 Trade = Technology

Trade allows countries to consume beyond their PPF, just like technology expands the PPF. Both increase productivity without new resources.

If India specializes in rice and China in cloth, both can consume MORE of both goods than if they each tried to produce everything — same as if technology improved.

Graph Mastery

Every graph you'll ever need — axes, shape, shifts, exam tricks, common mistakes.

Graph 1 — Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF)

When to draw: Any question with "construct PPF", "production combination", absolute/comparative advantage.

📐 How to Draw (Step-by-Step)

  1. Find Max of Good A: Total resources ÷ resources needed per unit of A
  2. Find Max of Good B: Total resources ÷ resources needed per unit of B
  3. Plot endpoints: (Max A, 0) on x-axis and (0, Max B) on y-axis
  4. Connect with straight line (constant OC = straight line PPF)
  5. Mark production point if they produce both goods (use % allocation)
Slope of PPF = −(Max of Y-axis good) ÷ (Max of X-axis good)
Slope magnitude = OC of x-axis good in terms of y-axis good
⚠️ Common Mistake
Don't plot "production hours" on the axes — plot the quantity of goods! Axes = Goods produced, not resources.
📌 70% Allocation Example
Austria: Max 40 cakes or 80 candles. 2000 hrs total.
70% to cakes → 1400 hrs → 1400/50 = 28 cakes
30% to candles → 600 hrs → 600/25 = 24 candles
Plot point (28, 24) on the PPF.

Straight line PPF = Constant opportunity cost. The slope of the line represents the opportunity cost of the x-axis good.

Graph 2 — Two PPFs on One Graph (Comparative Advantage)

When comparing two countries (e.g., India vs Japan), draw both PPFs on the same axes. The one with the less steep slope for a good has comparative advantage in that good.

How to Read:

  • If India's PPF is flatter for x-axis good → India has CA in x-good
  • If Japan's PPF is steeper → Japan has CA in y-good
  • The outer PPF (higher intercepts) = Absolute advantage in that good
📌 Exam Read
"Steeper slope of PPF = Higher OC = Comparative DISadvantage in that good". Always check BOTH intercepts to determine absolute advantage.

Graph 3 — Farmer vs Rancher PPF (Classic Textbook Example)

From your notes: Farmer takes 60 min/oz meat, 15 min/oz potato. Rancher takes 20 min/oz meat, 10 min/oz potato. Both work 8 hrs/day.

Farmer's PPF:

8 hrs = 480 min. Max meat = 480/60 = 8 oz. Max potato = 480/15 = 32 oz.

OC of meat = 4 potatoes/oz meat. OC of potato = 1/4 oz meat.

Rancher's PPF:

8 hrs = 480 min. Max meat = 480/20 = 24 oz. Max potato = 480/10 = 48 oz.

OC of meat = 2 potatoes/oz meat. OC of potato = 1/2 oz meat.

📌 Conclusion from Notes
Rancher has absolute advantage in BOTH (produces more of both). Farmer has CA in potatoes (OC = 1/4 < Rancher's 1/2). Rancher has CA in meat (OC = 2 < Farmer's 4). → Farmer specializes in potatoes, Rancher in meat. Both gain!

📐 PPF & Opportunity Cost Formulas

▼
Max Output of Good A = Total Resources ÷ Resources per unit of A
OC of Good A = Max Output B ÷ Max Output A
= Units of B sacrificed to produce 1 more unit of A

When to use: Any question asking to find comparative advantage, construct PPF, or determine basis of trade.

When NOT to use: Don't use this for CPI or GDP questions!

✅ Solved Example — Quiz Q2 (from image):

India: 40 hrs/motorcycle, 80 hrs/car | Japan: 60 hrs/motorcycle, 30 hrs/car

CountryOC of MotorcycleOC of Car
India80/40 = 2 cars40/80 = 0.5 motorcycles
Japan30/60 = 0.5 cars60/30 = 2 motorcycles

India has CA in Cars (OC=0.5 < Japan's 2). Japan has CA in Motorcycles (OC=0.5 < India's 2).

Absolute Advantage: India in Motorcycles (40 < 60 hrs). Japan in Cars (30 < 80 hrs).

📊 CPI Formulas

▼
Basket Cost (Year X) = Σ [Price(X) × Quantity(Base Year)]
Quantity is ALWAYS the base year quantity (fixed basket)
CPI = (Basket Cost in Current Year ÷ Basket Cost in Base Year) × 100
Inflation Rate (Year 2) = [(CPI Year 2 − CPI Year 1) ÷ CPI Year 1] × 100
Equivalent Current Value = Old Value × (CPI_current ÷ CPI_old)
⚠️ Critical Rule
When computing basket cost in ALL years, use the BASE YEAR QUANTITY. Do not use the current year quantity. This is the most common exam mistake!

✅ Solved Example — Quiz 2 Q1:

Sagar earned ₹70,000/month in 1990. CPI 1990 = 45, CPI 2026 = 270.

Equivalent salary in 2026 = 70,000 × (270 ÷ 45) = 70,000 × 6 = ₹4,20,000/month

🏦 GDP Formulas

▼
Nominal GDP = Σ (Current Year Price × Current Year Quantity)
Real GDP = Σ (Base Year Price × Current Year Quantity)
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP ÷ Real GDP) × 100
GDP Inflation Rate = [(Deflator Year 2 − Deflator Year 1) ÷ Deflator Year 1] × 100
GDP per Capita = GDP ÷ Total Population
⚡ Quick Rule
Nominal GDP Year 2024 with 2023 base year → use 2023 prices × 2024 quantities for Real GDP. GDP Deflator of base year = (NGDP_base ÷ RGDP_base) × 100 = 100 always.

✅ Solved Example — Assignment 2 Q1 (Partial):

2022 is base year. For year 2020: Milk 100 liters @ ₹40 current, ₹45 base price.

Nominal GDP 2020 = (100×40) + (200×50) + (300×100) = 4000+10000+30000 = ₹44,000

Real GDP 2020 = (100×45) + (200×50) + (300×150) = 4500+10000+45000 = ₹59,500

GDP Deflator 2020 = (44000÷59500) × 100 = 73.9 (Price level was lower than base year)

💵 Interest Rate Formulas

▼
Nominal Interest Rate = [(Final Amt − Initial Amt) ÷ Initial Amt] × 100
Real Interest Rate = Nominal Interest Rate − Inflation Rate
Inflation Rate = [(CPI_2 − CPI_1) ÷ CPI_1] × 100

✅ Solved Example — Assignment 1 Q5:

Deposit ₹2000 in 2024. In 2025, it becomes ₹2200. CPI: 200 → 220.

  • Nominal Rate = (2200−2000)/2000 × 100 = 10%
  • Inflation Rate = (220−200)/200 × 100 = 10%
  • Real Rate = 10% − 10% = 0% (purchasing power didn't change!)

✅ Solved Example — Assignment 1 Q6:

Lend ₹100, get ₹130. CPI increased by 20%.

  • Nominal Rate = (130−100)/100 × 100 = 30%
  • Inflation Rate = 20% (given)
  • Real Rate = 30% − 20% = 10%

💰 Saving & Investment Formulas

▼
Disposable Income = Y − T (Income minus Taxes)
Private Saving = Y − T − C
Public Saving = T − G (Positive = surplus, Negative = deficit)
National Saving = Private Saving + Public Saving = Y − C − G
Investment = National Saving (Closed Economy)

✅ Solved Example — Assignment 2 Q2:

GDP = $2,00,000 | C = $1,00,000 | G = $40,000 | Budget Surplus = $10,000 → T = G + Surplus = $50,000

  • Disposable Income = 2,00,000 − 50,000 = $1,50,000
  • Private Saving = 1,50,000 − 1,00,000 = $50,000
  • Public Saving = 50,000 − 40,000 = $10,000 (surplus)
  • National Saving = 50,000 + 10,000 = $60,000
  • Investment = $60,000

QUIZ 1 · Q1 Austria can produce max 40 cakes or 80 candles per hour. Total resources = 2000 labour hours. Before trade, allocates 70% to cakes, 30% to candles. Construct PPF and identify production combination. ▼

Concept Used: PPF Construction, Resource Allocation

  1. Find hourly rates: 40 cakes/hr means 1 cake takes 1/40 hr = 2000×40 = 80,000 cakes max... Wait — reread. Max 40 cakes OR 80 candles per hour means: 1 cake takes 1/40 hr, 1 candle takes 1/80 hr.
  2. Max output with 2000 hrs: Max Cakes = 2000 × 40 = 80,000 cakes | Max Candles = 2000 × 80 = 160,000 candles
  3. PPF endpoints: (80,000 cakes, 0) and (0, 160,000 candles). Draw straight line.
  4. 70% allocation: 70% of 2000 hrs = 1400 hrs for cakes → 1400 × 40 = 56,000 cakes | 30% = 600 hrs for candles → 600 × 80 = 48,000 candles
  5. Production point: Mark (56,000, 48,000) on the PPF line.
📌 PPF Slope Check
OC of 1 cake = 80,000/80,000... Actually OC = Max candles/Max cakes = 160,000/80,000 = 2 candles per cake. The PPF is downward sloping with slope = -2.
QUIZ 1 · Q2 India: 40 hrs/motorcycle, 80 hrs/car. Japan: 60 hrs/motorcycle, 30 hrs/car. Which country has absolute advantage in motorcycles and cars? ▼

Concept Used: Absolute Advantage — Lower hours per unit = Absolute Advantage

Japan6030

Absolute Advantage:

  • Motorcycles: India takes 40 hrs vs Japan's 60 hrs → India has AA in motorcycles
  • Cars: Japan takes 30 hrs vs India's 80 hrs → Japan has AA in cars

Bonus — Comparative Advantage:

CountryOC of MotorcycleOC of Car
India80/40 = 2 cars40/80 = 0.5 motorcycles
Japan30/60 = 0.5 cars60/30 = 2 motorcycles

QUIZ 2 · Q1 Sagar received ₹70,000/month salary in 1990. How much in 2026 if CPI 1990 = 45 and CPI 2026 = 270? ▼

Concept Used: Adjusting Wages for Inflation using CPI

Formula:

2026 Salary = 1990 Salary × (CPI_2026 ÷ CPI_1990)

Solution:
2026 Salary = 70,000 × (270 ÷ 45) = 70,000 × 6 = ₹4,20,000 per month

💡 Interpretation
This means ₹4,20,000 in 2026 has the SAME purchasing power as ₹70,000 in 1990. Prices are 6× higher, so you need 6× more money to maintain the same standard of living.
QUIZ 2 · Q2 GDP Deflator = 110 in 2024, base year 2023. Nominal GDP 2023 = $10,000, 2024 = $11,000. Analyse real GDP change. ▼

Concept Used: Real GDP using GDP Deflator

Real GDP = (Nominal GDP ÷ GDP Deflator) × 100

2023 (Base Year): Real GDP = (10,000 ÷ 100) × 100 = $10,000

2024: Real GDP = (11,000 ÷ 110) × 100 = $10,000

Analysis: Although Nominal GDP rose from $10,000 to $11,000, Real GDP stayed at $10,000. This means ALL the increase was due to price rise (inflation), NOT actual increase in production. The economy didn't really grow in real terms!

📌 Key Takeaway
When Real GDP doesn't change but Nominal GDP rises → economy is experiencing inflation with no real growth.
ASSIGN 1 · Q4 CPI calculation for Orange, Pizza, Wheat for years 2015-2017. Base basket: 40 kg Orange, 80 Pizza, 30 kg Wheat (2015 quantities). ▼

Concept Used: CPI Construction (5 Steps)

Step 1 — Fix Basket: 40 kg Orange, 80 Pizza, 30 kg Wheat (given 2015 base quantities)

Step 2 & 3 — Find Prices & Compute Basket Cost:

E-01 If CPI in 2024 is 150 and inflation rate is 8% between 2024 and 2025, what is the CPI in 2025? Easy ▼

Inflation Rate = [(CPI_2025 − CPI_2024) ÷ CPI_2024] × 100

8 = [(CPI_2025 − 150) ÷ 150] × 100

CPI_2025 = 150 + (8 × 150/100) = 150 + 12 = CPI 2025 = 162

E-02 In 1980, your uncle earned ₹25,000. CPI 1980 = 82, CPI 2025 = 246. What is the 2025 equivalent salary? Easy ▼

Equivalent salary = 25,000 × (246/82) = 25,000 × 3 = ₹75,000

E-03 Country A can produce 200 tons of rice or 100 m cloth. Country B can produce 150 tons of rice or 150 m cloth. Which has absolute advantage in rice? Easy ▼

Absolute advantage in rice: Compare max rice output. Country A (200) > Country B (150) → Country A has AA in rice. Country B has AA in cloth (150 > 100).

M-02 An American tourist spends ₹3,00,000 on hotels, food and travel in Goa in 2025. How does this affect India's 2025 GDP? Medium ▼

🔴 Hard Questions

H-01 "A country can enjoy absolute advantage and comparative advantage in both goods." Do you agree? Analyse. Hard ▼

ABSOLUTE Advantage in both — YES possible: If Country A is more efficient than B at producing both goods (fewer hours/unit for both), A has absolute advantage in both.

COMPARATIVE Advantage in both — IMPOSSIBLE: Each country must have CA in exactly one good. This is a mathematical certainty.

Proof by contradiction: If OC of good X in Country A < OC in Country B, then the reciprocal (OC of Y in A) must be > OC of Y in B. You can't have lower OC in both goods simultaneously.

Conclusion: A country CAN have absolute advantage in both goods, but CANNOT have comparative advantage in both goods. Trade is based on comparative advantage — even if one country is better at everything, both countries still gain by specializing.

📌 Example Answer
"I partially agree. Absolute advantage in both goods is possible (e.g., if Country A uses fewer labour hours for both goods). However, comparative advantage in both goods is mathematically impossible, as opportunity costs are reciprocal by nature."
H-02 "GDP is a good measure of economic well-being, but not a perfect one." Analyse. Hard ▼

Why GDP IS a good measure:

  • Strong correlation: High GDP per capita countries tend to have better healthcare, education, nutrition
  • Reflects productive capacity and living standards generally
  • Countries with higher GDP can afford better public services

Why GDP is NOT perfect (limitations):

  • Doesn't capture inequality — GDP may be high but concentrated among few (e.g., oil-rich nations with poor populations)
  • Leisure is excluded — A country working 80 hrs/week vs 40 hrs/week may have same GDP but very different well-being
  • Environmental degradation — Pollution-causing industries add to GDP but reduce quality of life
  • Non-market activities — Home cooking, voluntary work not counted
  • Underground economy — Informal sector excluded
  • Quality of life indices — Health, safety, freedom not measured

Conclusion: GDP is a useful first approximation but should be supplemented with Human Development Index (HDI), Gini Coefficient, etc. for complete picture of well-being.

⚡ Quick Calculation Tricks

PPF Intercepts in 2 seconds:

Max of Good A = Total Resources ÷ Cost per unit of A
Shortcut: If given "produces 40/hr with 2000 hrs" → 40 × 2000 = 80,000 units max.

CPI to salary adjustment:

New salary = Old salary × (New CPI ÷ Old CPI)
Shortcut: Find the ratio first (e.g., 270/45 = 6), then multiply.

Real vs Nominal GDP quick check:

If prices rose but Real GDP same → inflation with no growth.
If Real GDP rose → genuine economic growth.
Deflator = 100 always for base year.

Saving shortcut:

If Surplus given → T = G + Surplus
If Deficit given → T = G − Deficit
Investment = National Saving (always, in closed economy)

⚠️ Section 8

Confusion Breaker

The questions you were afraid to ask. Answered clearly.

🔵 Absolute Advantage

Compare RAW OUTPUT or RAW INPUT. Who produces more? Who uses fewer hours? Just compare the numbers directly.

Formula: Higher output per unit resource = AA (or fewer resources per unit output)

VS
🟣 Comparative Advantage

Compare OPPORTUNITY COST. Who gives up less? Requires calculation of OC for EACH good in EACH country.

Formula: OC of A = Max B ÷ Max A. Lower OC = CA in that good.

🔵 CPI

Fixed basket of goods (base year quantities stay same). Measures cost of living for CONSUMERS. Includes imports. Used for wage adjustments, salary comparisons.

VS
🟣 GDP Deflator

Changing basket (uses current production). Measures prices of ALL domestically produced goods. Excludes imports. Used to find Real GDP from Nominal.

🔵 Nominal GDP

Uses current year prices. Goes up even if production is flat (just prices rose). Does NOT reflect real growth.

VS
🟣 Real GDP

Uses base year prices. Shows TRUE change in economic output. If Real GDP rises, economy actually grew.

🔵 Private Saving

= Y − T − C. What households save after paying taxes and consuming. Can be positive or negative (dissaving).

VS
🟣 Public Saving

= T − G. What government saves from tax revenue after spending. Positive = surplus. Negative = deficit.

⚠️ Biggest Exam Traps
  • CPI basket quantity → ALWAYS use base year quantities, even when calculating other years' basket costs!
  • GDP Deflator of base year → Always = 100. If you get a different answer, you've made an error.
  • Comparative advantage → A country CANNOT have CA in BOTH goods. If your answer shows this, recalculate.
  • Government transfers (pensions, subsidies) → NOT part of G in GDP. Only actual government purchases count.
  • Intermediate goods → NOT counted in GDP directly (to avoid double counting). Use value-added method.
  • Second-hand sales → NOT part of GDP. Only NEW production counts.
  • LG sells 2024 inventory in 2025 → Counted in 2024 GDP, not 2025. Net effect on 2025 GDP = 0.
✅ Section 9

Quick Revision Notes

Read the night before. Ultra-compact exam summaries.

📦 PPF & Trade
  • PPF = straight line (constant OC)
  • OC of A = Max B ÷ Max A
  • Lower OC = Comparative Advantage
  • AA: more output, less resource
  • Both gain from CA-based trade
  • Specialization → consume beyond PPF
🛒 CPI
  • 5 steps: Fix basket → Prices → Cost → Base → Inflate
  • CPI = (Current Cost ÷ Base Cost) × 100
  • Base year CPI = 100
  • Fixed basket = base year quantities
  • 3 biases: Substitution, New goods, Quality
  • Salary adj = Old × (CPI_new ÷ CPI_old)
🏦 GDP
  • Y = C + I + G + NX
  • Nominal = Current prices × Current qty
  • Real = Base prices × Current qty
  • Deflator = (Nominal ÷ Real) × 100
  • Base year: Nominal = Real; Deflator = 100
  • Final goods only (no intermediate)
💵 Interest Rates
  • Nominal = (Final−Initial)/Initial × 100
  • Real = Nominal − Inflation
  • Inflation = [(CPI2−CPI1)/CPI1] × 100
  • Real rate shows true purchasing power gain
💰 Saving & Investment
  • Private Saving = Y − T − C
  • Public Saving = T − G
  • National Saving = Y − C − G
  • Investment = National Saving (closed)
  • Surplus → T > G → Public saving positive
  • Deficit → T < G → Public saving negative
📊 GDP Components
  • C: households buying final goods
  • I: firms, residential housing, inventory
  • G: government purchases (not transfers)
  • NX = Exports − Imports
  • Foreign spending in India = Export = +GDP
  • Own house = imputed rent = +C
⚠️ GDP Limitations
  • Ignores inequality distribution
  • Doesn't count leisure time
  • Environmental costs excluded
  • Informal economy excluded
  • Non-market activity not counted
  • Use HDI for fuller picture
🌱 Economic Growth
  • Sources: Physical K, Human K, Natural R, Tech
  • Trade = Technology effect on PPF
  • Capital accumulation: diminishing returns
  • GDP per capita = better living standard proxy
  • High GDP ≠ always high living standard
