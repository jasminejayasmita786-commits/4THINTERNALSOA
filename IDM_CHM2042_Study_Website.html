Core Concepts Explained

🌍 What is a Disaster?

WHO Definition: An occurrence that causes significant damage, ecological disruption, loss of human life, or deterioration of health services on a scale that necessitates an extraordinary response from external sources.

American Red Cross: An event (natural or man-made) causing human suffering and needs that victims cannot alleviate without assistance.

💡 Exam Tip: Always write BOTH definitions — WHO and Red Cross. Examiners love this. Mention the scale factor — it's what distinguishes disaster from a regular emergency.
🌪️

Natural Disasters

Caused by natural processes. Categories: Meteorological (floods, cyclones, blizzards), Topographical (earthquakes, landslides, volcanic eruptions), Environmental (global warming, ozone depletion, ENSO).

🏭

Man-Made / Anthropogenic

Caused by human actions. Types: Technological (transport failure, fire), Industrial (chemical/radioactive spills), Warfare (terrorism, civil unrest).

🔄 Integrated Disaster Management (IDM)

IDM is a comprehensive, holistic, and continuous approach covering all phases of the disaster cycle. Actions in one phase significantly affect outcomes in others.

🛡️

Mitigation

Reduce disaster effects BEFORE they occur. Building codes, zoning, vulnerability analyses, public education.

🎯

Preparedness

Activities PRIOR to disaster. Preparedness plans, emergency exercises, training, warning systems.

🚨

Response

Activities DURING disaster. Warning systems, emergency operations, search & rescue, food/water/medicine.

🏗️

Recovery

Activities AFTER disaster. Temporary housing, claims processing, grants, medical care, reconstruction.

⚠️ Exam Favourite: "Explain integrated disaster management with a schematic diagram." Draw the 4-phase cycle diagram — Mitigation → Preparedness → Response → Recovery → back to Mitigation.

⚠️ Hazard, Risk, Vulnerability & Capacity

TermMeaning
HazardA potentially dangerous phenomenon or event (e.g., earthquake, flood) that can cause loss of life or property damage.
VulnerabilityThe degree to which a population, community or system is unable to withstand the adverse effects of a hazard.
RiskRisk = Hazard × Vulnerability / Capacity. The probability of harmful consequences from a hazard event.
CapacityThe resources, skills and strengths available to reduce disaster risk or impact.
📐 Formula to Remember: Risk = Hazard × Vulnerability / Capacity. Higher vulnerability + lower capacity = higher risk.
💡 Exam Tip: "Discuss the relationship between vulnerability and hazard risk" — write the formula, then explain each component with an example (e.g., coastal community: hazard=cyclone, vulnerability=poor housing, capacity=early warning systems).

📊 SWOT Analysis in Disaster Management

SWOT = Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. It is used to evaluate an organization's capability to manage disasters effectively.

✅ Strengths

Internal positive factors. E.g., trained personnel, early warning systems, community awareness programs.

❌ Weaknesses

Internal negative factors. E.g., inadequate resources, poor infrastructure, lack of coordination.

🌟 Opportunities

External positive factors. E.g., NGO support, technology advancements, international aid frameworks.

⚡ Threats

External negative factors. E.g., increasing frequency of disasters, climate change, rapid urbanization.

💡 How SWOT helps: Identifies what resources you have (S), gaps to fill (W), what external help is available (O), and external dangers to prepare for (T). It gives a 360° view for disaster planning.

📜 Disaster Management Policy

A policy provides guidance and direction for decisions and actions to achieve specific outcomes. Key aims:

  • Guidance & Prioritization: Setting priorities for managing disaster situations
  • Community-Based Management: Promoting local-level execution
  • Capacity Development: Enhancing capabilities of all stakeholders
  • Inter-Agency Cooperation: Collaboration at national, regional, and international levels
  • Integration with Development: Incorporating disaster management into development planning
  • Disaster-Resilient Reconstruction: Building structures that can withstand future disasters
  • Time-Bound Actions: Ensuring prompt response, relief, rehabilitation, and reconstruction

🎯 Management by Objectives (MBO)

Introduced by Peter Drucker in 1954. MBO is a planning approach where specific organizational objectives are set collaboratively with clear accountability.

In disaster management, MBO ensures:

  • Clarity of Goals: Clear, accountable objectives fostering commitment
  • Synergy: Alignment between overall DM objectives and individual roles — "disaster management is everyone's business"
  • Timeliness: Objectives achieved within defined operational periods

MBO Guidelines: Set clear objectives → Plan action → Perform tasks → Periodic review → Evaluate performance → Reset for next cycle.

💡 Key Fact: Peter Drucker, 1954 — always mention this in the answer!

⚖️ Authority, Delegation and Decentralisation

Authority: The word 'authority' is used to mean power vested formally on an individual by a state. It gives the right to command, decide, and act.

Delegation: Delegation is the transfer of authority and responsibility to a person or position to carry out definite activities. It is the process of empowering a subordinate to make decisions on behalf of the authority. It saves response time but is a temporary arrangement.

💡 Why Delegation matters in DM: At times of disasters, all jobs cannot be carried out by a single individual, so tasks are allocated to subordinates. It also helps in enhancing skills and motivating subordinates.
📤

Delegation

Transfer of authority & responsibility to a subordinate. Temporary arrangement. Reduces response time. Enhances subordinate skills and motivation. E.g., a District Collector delegates flood relief coordination to BDO.

🌐

Decentralisation

Reallocation of duties and decision-making power away from core authority to ground-level personnel. Essential in disasters — anyone on ground level should have power to take decisions immediately without waiting for core authority.

🎯 Essence of Management: These are the essence of management and very vital from a disaster management perspective. Decentralisation ensures decisions are fast; delegation ensures tasks are completed efficiently.

🧑‍💼 Leadership & Management Skills

👑 Leadership Skills

Ensuring the right person is at the right place and right time during chaotic situations. Leaders take initiative, influence people, organize support, and motivate during pre/post-disaster phases.

🧠 Conceptual Skills

Gauging disaster reasons, understanding affected populations, visualizing situations, identifying root causes, and solving complex problems.

🤝 Human Relations Skills

⚙️ Managerial Skills

Managing available resources efficiently, coordinating, controlling situations, making quick decisions, and implementing them effectively.

🌀 Cyclone — Definition, Types & Formation

A cyclone is an intense atmospheric whirl with very strong winds circulating around a low-pressure center (eye). Derived from Greek Cyclos = "coils of a snake".

Wind circulation: Anti-clockwise in Northern Hemisphere, Clockwise in Southern Hemisphere.

🌊 Tropical Cyclones

Form over warm ocean near equator. Called Hurricanes (Atlantic/NE Pacific), Typhoons (NW Pacific), Cyclones (Indian Ocean/S Pacific).

❄️ Polar Cyclones

Occur in Greenland, Siberia, Antarctica. Usually stronger in winter months.

⚡ Mesocyclones

Form when part of a thunderstorm starts to spin, potentially leading to a tornado. "Meso" = middle.

Mechanism of Cyclone Formation (7 Steps):

  1. Warm moist air over ocean rises → creates low pressure below
  2. Cooler air from surroundings flows into low-pressure zone
  3. New air becomes warm/moist, rises → cycle continues
  4. As warm air rises and cools → water vapour forms clouds
  5. System of clouds and wind grows fed by ocean heat & evaporation
  6. As storm rotates faster → eye forms at centre
  7. Becomes Tropical Storm at 63 km/h; Tropical Cyclone at 119 km/h
⚠️ Common Mistake: Students confuse Rayleigh wave with cyclone terms. Rayleigh wave belongs to EARTHQUAKES, not cyclones!

🌍 Earthquake — Types & Seismic Waves

Shaking of Earth's surface from a sudden release of energy in the lithosphere, creating seismic waves.

Key Terms:

  • Focus/Hypocenter: Point on fault where rupture occurs
  • Epicenter: Point on surface directly above focus
  • Fault Line: Break/fracture where plates move
  • Seismology: Scientific study of earthquakes

4 Types of Earthquakes:

  • Tectonic — most common & disastrous
  • Volcanic — near volcanic activity
  • Collapse — mines/underground caverns
  • Explosion — nuclear/chemical detonations

⛰️ Landslide — Definition & Causes

A landslide is the mass movement of rock, debris, or earth down a slope due to gravity. Triggered by natural or human-induced factors.

Major Causes:

  • Heavy/prolonged rainfall
  • Earthquakes causing slope failure
  • Volcanic eruptions
  • Snowmelt on steep slopes
  • Deforestation and vegetation removal
  • Mining and excavation activities
  • Urbanization on unstable slopes
  • Weakening of slope soil/rock structure

💧 Floods — Causes & Management

A flood is an overflow of water where the flow rate exceeds channel capacity, breaks levees, or accumulates on saturated ground.

Major Causes of Floods:

  • Heavy precipitation (primary cause)
  • Bank erosion and silting (reduces channel capacity)
  • Landslides blocking rivers
  • Poor drainage systems
  • Tidal/backwater effects (coastal regions)
  • Snowmelt and glacial outbursts
  • Encroachment in flood plains

Structural vs Non-Structural Flood Management:

🏗️ Structural

Dams & reservoirs (e.g., Hirakud Dam on Mahanadi), channel improvement, embankments, river diversion, anti-erosion works, inter-basin transfer.

📋 Non-Structural

Flood forecasting & warning, flood plain zoning, flood proofing structures, flood insurance, relief & rehabilitation.

India Stats: Total flood-prone area = 40 million hectares (~12% of India's area). Most affected: UP, Bihar, Assam, West Bengal, Odisha.

Concurrent: Real-time adjustment during response.

FSNP: Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing. Each stage builds group cohesion. Performing = maximum disaster management effectiveness.

Authority vs Delegation vs Decentralisation

Delegation: Transfer of authority to subordinate — temporary.
Decentralisation: Distribution of decision-making power to ground level. In disasters: decentralisation is essential for immediate action.

🎯

Preparedness — 3 Main Elements

(1) Forecasting events and issuing warnings.
(2) Taking precautionary measures in response to warnings.
(3) Improving response by organizing and strengthening capacity. Aim = Reduction of disaster impact.

🗂️

Contingency Planning — 3 Stages

Plan B for when primary plans fail. Readiness → Standby → Activation. Must address all possible scenarios, clearly state roles, be communicated to all team members, and be accessible to the community.

🚨

Aims of Disaster Response

Save lives · Provide food/water/medicine/shelter · Restore critical services · Manage secondary threats (fire, disease) · Coordinate resources · Protect vulnerable groups. "Medicine, food, water, shelter" — core response deliverables.

🏗️

Recovery — Medium vs Long Term

Medium: Debris removal, restore services, temporary housing, medical care (weeks–months).
Long: Permanent reconstruction, economic rehabilitation, social rebuilding, disaster-resilient build-back (months–years).

📺

Role of Media in DM

Early warning · Public awareness · Relief coordination · Government accountability · Fundraising. Negative: Sensationalism causes panic. Media must report responsibly and accurately during disasters.

🧠

Triage — 4 Colour Codes

Red = Immediate (life-threatening). Yellow = Delayed (serious, not critical). Green = Minor (walking wounded). Black = Dead/expectant. Prioritises care when resources are limited.

🌿

Environment & Disaster Link

Deforestation → Landslides + Floods. Wetland destruction → Floods. Climate change → Extreme weather. Soil erosion → River bank failures. Protecting nature = reducing disaster risk.

💬

Psycho-Social Phases After Disaster

4 phases: Acute (self-survival) → Reaction (rescue/aid others) → Recovery (DM teams act) → Reorientation (acceptance). Counseling is most needed in Recovery and Reorientation phases.

PYQ Mid-25 ▼

  • Hazard: The threatening event (e.g., cyclone, earthquake)
  • Vulnerability: Susceptibility of population to that hazard (poor housing, poverty, location)

2(a) How can SWOT analysis help in effective management of a disaster?
PYQ Mid-25 ▼
📝 2 Marks

SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) provides a 360° analysis framework for disaster management:

  • Strengths: Identify existing resources — trained personnel, early warning systems, community preparedness programs. Helps leverage these during disasters.
  • Weaknesses: Reveal gaps — inadequate funds, poor infrastructure, coordination failures. Allows corrective action before disaster strikes.
  • Opportunities: External advantages — NGO networks, international aid, new technology (GIS, satellite). Allows better planning and resource acquisition.
  • Threats: External risks — climate change, rapid urbanization, political instability. Helps prepare contingency plans.

How it helps: SWOT enables planners to make data-driven decisions by matching strengths to opportunities, converting weaknesses to strengths, and preparing for threats. It is a major component of effective DM as it provides a realistic picture before, during, and after a disaster.

4(a) Define and classify cyclone. Discuss major causes and mechanism of cyclone formation.
PYQ Mid-25 ▼
📝 2 Marks

Definition: A cyclone is an intense atmospheric whirl with very strong winds circulating around a low-pressure center (the "eye"). The word comes from Greek "Cyclos" meaning coils of a snake.

Classification:

  • Tropical Cyclones: Form over warm tropical oceans. Called Hurricanes (Atlantic), Typhoons (NW Pacific), Cyclones (Indian Ocean)
  • Polar Cyclones: Occur in Greenland, Siberia, Antarctica. Stronger in winter.
  • Mesocyclones: Form from rotating thunderstorms; may lead to tornadoes.

  1. Warm moist air over ocean rises → low pressure area created
  2. Cooler surrounding air rushes in to fill the low pressure
  3. This air warms up, becomes moist, and rises again (cycle repeats)
  4. Rising air cools and condenses → forms clouds
  5. System grows, fed by ocean heat and evaporation
  6. Coriolis force causes rotation → eye forms at centre
  7. Classified as cyclone when winds exceed 119 km/h

5(b) Differentiate nuclear fission and fusion. Write the principle of energy generation.
PYQ Mid-25 ▼
📝 2 Marks

Nuclear Fission: Splitting of a heavy atomic nucleus (like U-235 or Pu-239) into two smaller nuclei when struck by a neutron, releasing enormous energy + more neutrons (chain reaction).

Nuclear Fusion: Two light nuclei (like deuterium + tritium — hydrogen isotopes) combine at extremely high temperatures to form a heavier nucleus (helium), releasing even greater energy.

Principle of Energy Generation (E = mc²):

In both fission and fusion, there is a small loss of mass (called mass defect). This lost mass is converted to energy according to Einstein's equation: E = mc² where m = mass defect, c = speed of light (3×10⁸ m/s). Even tiny mass converts to enormous energy.

5(c) Write the major reasons for Chernobyl Accident and its consequences.
PYQ Mid-25 ▼
📝 2 Marks

Major Reasons (April 26, 1986 — Ukraine, USSR):

  1. A safety test was conducted at low power level — an inherently unstable operating zone for RBMK reactors
  2. The reactor had a positive void coefficient design flaw — as steam formed, reactivity increased (opposite of safe design)
  3. Operators disabled multiple safety systems to conduct the test without interruption
  4. Sudden uncontrolled power surge → steam explosion → graphite fire
  5. The reactor had no containment structure, allowing massive radiation release

Consequences:

  • Massive release of radioactive particles over Europe (caesium-137, iodine-131)
  • 30-km exclusion zone created (still largely uninhabited)
  • 31 immediate deaths; thousands of cancer cases attributed (especially thyroid cancer in children)
  • 350,000+ people permanently evacuated from Belarus and Ukraine
  • Estimated $700 billion in total cost (cleanup, health, economic)
  • Led to global nuclear safety reforms, IAEA protocol revisions, and acceleration of Soviet Union's collapse

2. Explain the relationship between hazard, risk, vulnerability and capacity.
2 Marks ▼
📝 2 Marks

Formula: Risk = Hazard × Vulnerability / Capacity

  • Hazard: A dangerous phenomenon that can cause loss (e.g., cyclone, earthquake).

  • Capacity: Resources and strengths available to cope with hazard (e.g., early warning systems, trained teams).
  • Risk: Probability of harmful consequences. Risk increases when vulnerability is high and capacity is low.
Example: Coastal village → Hazard = Cyclone, Vulnerability = Kutcha houses, Capacity = Cyclone shelter + warning system. Higher capacity = Lower risk.

  • Looting and theft: Abandoned homes and relief supply convoys may be targeted by opportunists.

  • Fraud in relief distribution: Corruption and misallocation of relief materials.
  • Trafficking: Vulnerable displaced persons, especially women and children, may be exploited.

🏗️ Chapter 6 — Disaster Recovery

1. Discuss major medium- and long-term recovery aspects with examples.

  • Debris removal and site clearance.

3. What is Triage? How can it help in classifying injuries?
2 Marks ▼
📝 2 Marks

Triage is a medical system for sorting patients during mass casualty events to prioritize treatment based on urgency and survivability when resources are limited.

Classification system (colour-coded):

  • Red (Immediate): Life-threatening injuries that can be treated — top priority. E.g., severe bleeding, airway obstruction.
  • Yellow (Delayed): Serious injuries but not immediately life-threatening. Can wait. E.g., fractures without circulatory compromise.
  • Green (Minor): Walking wounded — minor injuries needing basic treatment. E.g., small cuts, bruises.
  • Black (Expectant/Deceased): Fatal injuries or death. No further resources allocated.

6. What is a hazard map? Explain with an example.
2 Marks ▼
📝 2 Marks

A hazard map is a spatial representation (map) that shows areas at risk from specific hazards — identifying the geographic extent, severity, and probability of occurrence of hazards.

Uses: Land-use planning, risk communication, evacuation planning, prioritizing mitigation investments.

Example — India's Seismic Hazard Map: India is divided into Zones II to V. Zone V (dark red on the map) covers Himalayan region, NE India, Bhuj, Andaman & Nicobar Islands — highest seismic hazard. Civil engineers use this map to design earthquake-resistant structures in these zones.

7. Explain the relationship between environmental degradation and disaster.
2 Marks ▼
📝 2 Marks

Environmental degradation increases vulnerability to disasters through multiple mechanisms:

  • Deforestation → Landslides and Floods: Trees stabilize slopes and absorb water. Without them, heavy rain causes landslides and flash floods (e.g., Uttarakhand 2013).
  • Wetland destruction → Floods: Wetlands act as natural sponges. Draining them increases flood severity in downstream areas.
  • Climate change → Extreme weather: GHG emissions from industrial pollution drive global warming, increasing cyclone intensity and unpredictable rainfall.
  • Soil erosion → River bank failures: Unsustainable agriculture leads to silting of rivers, reducing flood-carrying capacity.

Conclusion: Protecting the environment is fundamentally a disaster risk reduction strategy.

2 Marks ▼
📝 2 Marks

The process involves understanding the 4 emotional phases disaster victims go through:

  • Acute state: Individuals focus on self-survival — saving own life and family.
  • Reaction stage: Individuals participate in search and rescue, provide medical care to others.
  • Recovery phase: DM teams take control. Psychological needs (grief counseling, trauma therapy) become prominent.

Needs are identified through direct observation, community surveys, interviews with survivors, and health worker reports at relief camps.
